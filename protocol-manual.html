Greeting Protocols

How you enter and exit a room sets the tone for everything that follows.

1 The Entry Greeting
Upon entering your Queen's presence, pause at the threshold. Make eye contact. Bow your head briefly — not a nod, a genuine bow. Speak: "Good [morning/afternoon/evening], my Queen." Wait to be acknowledged before proceeding further.
2 The Kneeling Greeting
For formal occasions or when you have been apart for more than twelve hours: kneel upon entry. Both knees. Back straight. Hands on thighs, palms up. Head bowed. Wait in this position until your Queen speaks or touches you.
3 The Departure
Never leave your Queen's presence without acknowledgment. Request: "My Queen, may I be excused?" Upon receiving permission: bow, thank her, and depart without turning your back until you reach the door. Face her as long as you can.
4 Public Adaptation
In public, adapt these protocols to be invisible to outsiders. The bow becomes a subtle head-tilt. The kneeling greeting becomes a hand on your heart. The departure request becomes a quiet "I'll be right back." The energy remains; the visibility adjusts.

Address & Speech

Words shape reality. How you speak to and about your Queen defines your dynamic.

5 Forms of Address
In private: "my Queen," "my Owner," or your negotiated honorific. Never her given name without explicit permission. In public: use her preferred public name or title. The honorific is a private gift — protect it from outsider ears.
6 Answering
When your Queen asks a direct question, begin your response with "Yes, my Queen" or "No, my Queen." This small acknowledgment reinforces your roles with every exchange.
7 Self-Reference
In formal settings, refer to yourself in the third person or by your role: "your servant," "your pet," "this one." Not for degradation — for clarity. It reminds both of you of the structure you've chosen.
8 Gratitude
Thank your Queen for: corrections, permissions, attention, gifts, and presence. Even difficult moments deserve gratitude — they are opportunities for growth. "Thank you for correcting me, my Queen. I will do better."

Positions

Physical positions create mental states. Train these until they become instinctive.

9 Present
Standing: feet shoulder-width, hands clasped behind back, shoulders back, chin level, eyes forward. This is your neutral waiting position. Practice it until it feels natural. You will be inspected from time to time — be ready.
10 Kneel
Both knees on the ground, back straight, hands on thighs with palms up, head bowed slightly. This is the position of supplication, greeting, and formal address. Hold it without fidgeting. Your stillness is your offering.
11 Rest
Seated or lying at your Queen's feet. This is not a punishment position. It is a comfort position — for when she wants you close but not formal. You may read, doze, or simply be near her. Physical proximity is itself a protocol.
12 Display
Standing or kneeling in a way that presents your body for inspection or admiration. Your Queen will position you as she wishes. Hold the position without adjusting. You are being viewed. That is the entire purpose.

Service Standards

Service is the daily expression of your devotion. These standards apply to every task.

13 Drink Service
Know your Queen's preferences by heart. Morning coffee: temperature, strength, additions. Evening tea: steep time, cup choice. Water: with or without ice, still or sparkling. Offer on a tray. Kneel when presenting. "Your coffee, my Queen."
14 Meal Service
Your Queen eats first. Always. You do not lift your utensils until she has taken her first bite. You do not finish your meal until she has finished hers. Offer to clear her plate. Thank her for allowing you to share her table.
15 Dressing Service
When permitted to assist with dressing: lay out garments in the order they will be worn. Offer each piece with both hands. Kneel when helping with shoes. Speak only to confirm preferences: "The navy or the black, my Queen?"
16 Bath Service
Draw the bath at the correct temperature. Test it with your elbow — not your hand. Arrange towels, robe, and products within reach. Light candles if your Queen prefers them. Announce when ready. Withdraw or stay as instructed. Your presence is at her discretion.

Discipline & Correction

Correction is not punishment. It is realignment. Receive it with gratitude.

17 Receiving Correction
When corrected: make eye contact, acknowledge with "Understood, my Queen," and state what you will do differently. Do not explain. Do not justify. Do not deflect. The correction is a gift. Receive it as one.
18 Formal Punishment
For serious infractions, your Queen may prescribe formal punishment. Accept the position she assigns. Count if instructed. Thank her after each element. The punishment ends when she says it ends — not when you've had enough.
19 After Correction
Once the correction is complete, the matter is closed. Your Queen will signal this — usually with a touch, a word, or permission to rise. Do not re-litigate the infraction. Do not dwell in shame. You have been corrected. You are clean. Move forward.

Rituals & Routines

Daily rituals anchor your dynamic in the ordinary moments of life.

20 Morning Ritual
Rise before your Queen. Prepare her morning drink. Kneel beside the bed or at a designated spot. When she wakes: "Good morning, my Queen. How may I serve you today?" Wait for her first instructions. Your day begins when hers does.
21 Evening Ritual
At day's end, kneel before your Queen and offer a brief accounting: what you accomplished, what you struggled with, what you're grateful for. End with: "I exist to serve you. Goodnight, my Queen." She may dismiss you or keep you close.
22 Weekly Review
Once per week — same day, same time — your Queen conducts a formal review. She will assess your service, your growth, your areas for improvement. Prepare your own reflections. This is a conversation, not a monologue. Be honest about what's working and what isn't.

Conduct & Deportment

How you carry yourself reflects on your Queen. You are her representative.

23 Posture
Stand tall. Shoulders back. Chin level. Never slouch in your Queen's presence — it signals disrespect for her and for yourself. Good posture is a gift you give to everyone who sees you. Your Queen especially.
24 Eye Contact
In formal settings, lower your eyes when addressed. In conversation, maintain respectful eye contact — not staring, not avoiding. This balance takes practice. When in doubt, ask: "My Queen, may I look at you?"
25 Silence
Do not fill silence with noise. Stillness is a skill. When your Queen is thinking, reading, or resting, be invisible in your presence. You are near but not demanding. The silence is shared, not empty.
